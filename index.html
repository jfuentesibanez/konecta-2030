01

Summary for the committee

Konecta is, at the same time, one of the companies most exposed to agentic AI in the world and one of the best positioned to lead it. Those two things will not stay in balance for long.

The core business — human interactions at scale — is exactly what AI agents are learning to do. But Konecta has what no AI lab has: the operational knowledge of millions of real interactions, enterprise relationships with ~500 clients, presence in the last mile of the customer relationship, and a proprietary platform (Kolibri) already in production.

The three decisions that cannot wait

1. Proactively cannibalize key accounts with Konecta's own AI — before someone else does.
2. Decide whether Kolibri is an internal tool or the next P&L line.
3. Negotiate the workforce transition at scale while there is still time and cash to do it well.

This report is not a closed recommendation: it is a navigation instrument. Every forecast is auditable, every figure carries a label (FACT INFERENCE HYPOTHESIS) and the scenarios are recalibrated quarterly against observable signals.

02

The thesis

For twenty years, outsourced customer experience has been sold by the hour of human work. Agentic AI breaks the equation: the marginal cost of a resolved interaction tends to zero, and with it, the per-FTE pricing model. This is not the incremental automation the industry already knows (IVR, chatbots, RPA): it is the first technology able to complete the interaction end-to-end.

The comforting analogy — "the cloud took twenty years and only 25% migrated" — deserves to be taken seriously, and precisely for that reason, stress-tested. Adopting AI agents requires no capex, no system migration, no vendor switch. It is bought by API and paid by outcome. And the one barrier that used to protect outsourcers — integration with legacy client systems — is being dissolved by computer use: models that read screens and click through AS400/Citrix like a human does. ✓ DR3

The market has already voted — before the P&L did ✓ DR1

Public-market investors are not waiting for BPO revenues to fall; they are re-pricing the sector's right to exist. Teleperformance trades at ~€3.3B market cap on ~€10.2B revenue (≈0.3× sales) after a −29.3% single-day crash when Klarna announced its OpenAI assistant. TTEC's market cap: ~$114M. TaskUs: from a $1.5-1.6B take-private valuation to ~$450M in about a year — while its AI Services grow 36%. Short interest on TP, Concentrix and TTEC kept rising into 2026. FACT

Meanwhile, Gartner projects 80% of common service issues resolved autonomously by 2029 — yet found only 20% of organizations had actually cut agent headcount by 2026. That gap between what the market has priced and what operations have executed is the window this report is about. It is Konecta's greatest risk (the multiple compresses anyway) and its greatest opportunity (execution is still open). FACT + INFERENCE

Timeline 2026 → 2030

A phase-by-phase narrative with verifiable milestones — not a prediction, but the central trajectory that the signals in section 07 will confirm or refute quarter by quarter. ✓ calibrated: tech DR3 · market DR1 · economics DR2 · regulation DR4

SECOND HALF OF 2026

The tense calm

The 2024-25 pilots move into production. Voice agents now answer below the human perception threshold (<300ms) and handle interruptions naturally; agentic reliability on 3-5 step tasks has reached 82% on τ-bench — but collapses to 48% beyond 8 steps. That gap defines what gets automated first. The first pay-per-resolution contracts appear in renewals — Crescendo publishes $1.25 per resolution; Transcom sells outcome-based models on FCR/CSAT. Clients move too: Santander targets 40% of UK call volume via AI self-service; Home Depot rolls out a Gemini voice agent nationally. In Konecta, Kolibri captures use cases faster than the organization can prioritize them — the bottleneck is no longer the technology. Two regulatory clocks start ticking: the AI Act's transparency obligations (Art. 50) become effective in August 2026, and Spain's contact-center collective agreement (III Convenio) expires in December 2026 — unions are expected to demand "AI layoff vetoes" and human quotas in the next one. ✓ DR3 · DR1 · DR4

FIRST HALF OF 2027

First cracks

Renewals arrive with explicit deflation — already observed at −15/−25% of contract value on automatable scopes when clients bring their own stack. Software gets cheaper too: Microsoft ran a 40% discount on Dynamics Contact Center — a land grab that compresses the whole value pool. A global competitor announces the first major workforce reduction citing automation. Open-weight models deployable on-premise match the 2024 frontier — and European banks start asking for sovereign deployments as a contract condition. The question in every client QBR shifts from "what are you doing with AI?" to "why am I still paying for hours?" ✓ DR3 · DR1

SECOND HALF OF 2027 · ⚠ THE FORK

The decision

The moment when protecting current revenue and building future revenue stop being compatible. Cannibalizing your own account with AI costs revenue today; not doing it hands that account to a third party tomorrow. What the committee decides in the 12 months before this point determines which of the three scenarios materializes. This is where the report stops being a story and becomes yours: the simulator (section 05) lets you choose your beliefs and see their consequences.

2028 · KATALYST CHECKPOINT

Divergence

The year of the €2.5B target. In S2 it is reached — but with an unrecognizable mix: pay-per-resolution, platform licensing and liability-as-a-service (charging to own the legal risk of AI errors) offset the erosion of voice. Machine-to-machine endpoints appear: the client's AI and the company's AI settle disputes in milliseconds via API, because forcing consumer agents through human IVRs breaks operations. In S1 the target is reached nominally or missed — and it barely matters: the valuation multiple has already compressed, because the market doesn't pay for revenue, it pays for mix. ✓ DR3

2029 → 2030

Four possible Konectas

The fog clears. By 2029, ~72% of historical interaction volume is automatable end-to-end at ≥ human quality (central estimate). The global CX/BPO sector has reorganized: those who cannibalized in time operate fleets of AI agents — one human overseeing 25+ — with people concentrated in the high-value, high-empathy work; those who didn't compete in a price auction over a shrinking human volume. ⧉ Presentation version — slide by slide

Three ideas.

1

The unit of this business is changing. For twenty years the industry sold hours of people; the market is starting to buy resolved outcomes. What is being redistributed is not technology — it is pricing power. Whoever prices the new unit defines the new market.

2

Capability is not deployed reality. The models can already do far more than enterprises actually run in production. Everything valuable in the next four years happens inside that gap — and that gap is exactly the width of the window.

3

The scarce asset is not intelligence — it is the right to operate it. AI answers are heading toward free; owning the risk, the compliance and the trust is not. The business that survives sells accountability, with AI inside.

Five questions.

1
Pricing

In your next six renewals, who sets the price of an automated interaction — you, or the client's procurement?

Where to look: per-resolution clauses and savings-sharing demands in the 2026-27 renewal pipeline.

2
Clients

Which of your top-10 clients already has an internal conversational-AI team — and what will they still need you for in 2028?

Where to look: client job postings, QBR agendas, volumes quietly moving to self-service.

3
Platforms

When agents ship inside the licenses your clients already pay for, what exactly do they still buy from Konecta?

Where to look: the share of contracts where Konecta owns orchestration and data — not just the labor.

4
People

Can natural attrition outrun the transition — and what happens to the people who stay, handling only the hardest interactions?

Where to look: monthly attrition vs. the required reduction rate; pay, status and burnout in escalation teams.

5
Kolibri

Is it a tool or a business — and would you sell it to someone who competes with your own operations?

Where to look: the "Sell" pipeline, and whether Kolibri has a price list by Q1 2027.

Seven trends.

1

Outcome pricing goes mainstream — $1.25 per resolution is already public.

Red flag: a top-10 client RFP admits per-resolution bids only.

2

Voice is getting solved — <300ms latency, natural interruptions, any accent.

Red flag: a voice agent runs 1M+ retention calls/month at NPS parity.

3

Computer use dissolves the integration moat — AI clicks through AS400/Citrix like a human.

Red flag: a client deploys agents on legacy systems with no integration project.

4

Platforms bundle agents as loss leaders — 40% license discounts already seen.

Red flag: automated interactions arrive "free" inside a major CCaaS renewal.

5

Customers start sending their own agents — the contact center goes machine-to-machine.

Red flag: >2% of inbound identified as synthetic callers.

6

Regulation hardens into a moat — AI Act, right-to-a-human, ISO 42001 in tenders.

Red flag: the first Tier-1 RFP disqualifies non-certified vendors.

7

The human premium becomes a product — Klarna re-hired; paid human tiers are coming.

Red flag: a major brand charges extra for guaranteed human service.

Three ideas to argue with. Five questions only this committee can answer. Seven trends nobody controls. They collide in one place.

06

The four scenarios

There were three scenarios in this report — until the cross-model red team ran. Both frontier models, independently, flagged the same missing future (S4), and both cut the weight of Metamorphosis. Weights below are the post-red-team synthesis; the votes are shown transparently. They are not predictions: they are probability masses that the signals in section 07 will shift every quarter.

35%

S1 · Erosion

The reaction comes late. Konecta protects its current revenue; clients in-source or migrate to disruptors; renewals enter a price war. The end state is the worst negotiating position a BPO can hold: clients keep the AI layer and buy only "human fallback as a utility" ✓DR1. In 2030: similar nominal revenue, compressed margin, a declining BPO multiple, and a workforce transition done under duress — expensive, rushed, and without the cash to negotiate it well.

30%

S2 · Metamorphosis

Cannibalize yourself before others do. Konecta proactively migrates its accounts to its own AI agents priced per resolution, turns Kolibri into a business line and its operational knowledge into the moat. The move is proven in both directions: Crescendo (AI-native) bought a BPO to industrialize it; TaskUs (BPO) shifted its mix until traditional CX is only ~45% of revenue ✓DR1. And hyper-regulation becomes the moat: DORA + AI Act compliance is so onerous it expels pure AI startups from corporate B2B — Konecta, already ISO 42001-certified, sells banks the legal shield to deploy AI without owning the risk ✓DR4. The margin prize is real: CX software runs at 55-66% gross margin vs ~15% for BPO — Kolibri-as-product is the claim on that layer ✓DR2. In 2030: a smaller but far more qualified workforce, an unrecognizable mix, higher margin, and the algorithmic-humanism thesis turned into competitive advantage — humans exactly where they add the most value.

15%

S3 · Human Fortress

Adoption disappoints. Reliability, regulation and customer preference slow deep automation; human interaction gains a value premium. This scenario has real evidence behind it: Klarna partially re-hired humans after quality eroded; only 20% of organizations had actually cut agent headcount by 2026; and Spain's draft law would make AI-only service illegal, mandating a human within 3 minutes ✓DR1 · DR4. In 2030: human BPO endures as a premium category, and whoever kept capacity and quality — while still automating the basics — captures that premium. The risk of this scenario is not living it: it is using it as an excuse not to decide. The sharpest version of this play, from the red team: become the global monopoly on the hardest 15-50% — grave complaints, borderline fraud, vulnerable customers, litigation risk — the volume AI can never legally own, priced accordingly.

20%

S4 · Platform Capture

Konecta executes well — and still loses the valuable layer. Salesforce, Google, Microsoft and the CCaaS vendors ship native agents inside the licenses where client data already lives. Clients neither in-source nor buy Konecta's AI: they switch a module on. Konecta transforms, grows "AI-led" revenue — and becomes a channel: an implementer and operator of someone else's platform, keeping service margin while the platform keeps product, data, pricing and roadmap. The uncomfortable edge: the Google alliance that powers Konecta's capabilities is also the single most plausible path into this scenario. Both red-team models proposed this future independently. △ RT ×2

Weight votes — GPT 5.6 SOL: 40/30/10/20 · Gemini Deep Think: 50/15/15/20 · synthesis shown: 35/30/15/20. Red-teamers structurally over-discount incumbent agency; Konecta's real assets (ISO 42001, Kolibri in production, ~500 enterprise relationships) justify S2 above the red-team average. The committee should debate exactly this delta.

The stress test of algorithmic humanism

All four scenarios take the same hypothesis seriously: that empathy, judgment and human connection have lasting economic value. The difference between S2 and S1 is not believing or not believing in the human — it is whether that belief becomes an active strategy (choosing where humans win and automating the rest) or a passive alibi (waiting for the market to confirm it). The simulator lets you find the exact point where one turns into the other.

08

Decisions for the committee

Six dilemmas with no technical answer. No analysis resolves them: they are leadership choices. Each with its decision gate — the date after which not deciding is deciding.

D1 · Cannibalization gate: Q4 2026

Do we proactively migrate 2-3 top accounts to our own AI agent priced per resolution, accepting the short-term revenue loss? Who picks the accounts: us, or the competitor who gets there first?

D2 · Kolibri gate: Q1 2027

Internal efficiency tool or product with its own P&L? The menu already says "Sell". If it is a product: do we also sell it to companies that compete with Konecta in operations?

D3 · Build or buy gate: continuous

The AI-native CX disruptors are still buyable — but the window is narrowing: the best assets are being absorbed by software vendors and PE, not by BPOs (Zendesk bought Forethought; Decagon tendered at $4.5B; Sierra at $10B) ✓DR1. Do we acquire capability (technology + talent + logos) or trust our partner route (Google, CrewAI, Uniphore)? What happens if a competitor buys first?

D4 · The workforce transition gate: before the IV Convenio negotiation (Dec 2026)

The research is blunt on this one. Mass reskilling at scale has historically failed: Spanish banking redeployed <10% of 115,000 exits internally; the realistic BPO redeployment rate is 10-15%. Meanwhile a forced ERE in Spain invites judicial audit of the algorithms and costs €15-30k per exit (Telefónica paid €380-455k). The viable instrument is silent attrition: the sector's natural 30-40% annual rotation, unreplenished, can absorb the whole transition at near-zero exit cost — if it starts early. Delay compresses the required reduction rate above natural attrition, and every excess exit is paid at full legal price. ✓DR4

So the real questions: Is €20M/3yr of reskilling an operational plan or an ESG narrative — and which do we need it to be? When do we freeze net hiring, and in which geographies first (the friction map says: automate via Egypt/India, humanize via Spain)? And for those who remain handling the hardest 15% of interactions: pay, status and psychological support must be redesigned, or burnout empties the fortress. One tactical rule from the evidence: never announce headcount savings from AI — Klarna's press releases became legal and reputational ammunition. ✓DR3 · DR4

D5 · The price gate: Q2 2027

Do we lead the transition to outcome-based pricing — including liability-as-a-service, charging to own the legal risk of AI errors — or defend the per-FTE model until clients break it? Whoever sets the new price defines the new market.

D6 · The Katalyst question gate: next board cycle

The red team's hardest attack deserves to be faced head-on: you cannot lead a massive deflation of your core business unit and simultaneously promise the top line grows from €2,030M to €2,500M. The simulator agrees: under central calibration, none of the four scenarios reaches €2.5B by 2028. Successful Metamorphosis may well mean a smaller, more profitable, more valuable Konecta — higher margin, higher multiple, lower revenue. △ RT

So the question is not "how do we reach €2.5B?" but "is revenue the right target variable for 2028 — or should Katalyst's headline metric become EBITDA, mix, or enterprise value?" A target that can only be met by re-labeling revenue as AI-led (see F4) would be worse than a smaller honest one. This is the committee's decision to make — the report's job is to make sure it is made consciously, not by inertia.

Method and sources

This report was built through a deliberately adversarial multi-model process:

1. Deep research — four independent deep-research runs: market & competition + economics of disruption (GPT 5.6 SOL); technology trajectory + regulation & labor (Gemini Deep Think). Status: all four received and integrated.

2. Synthesis and modeling — consolidation, scenarios, simulator and writing (Claude Fable 5).

3. Cross-model red team — each model attacked the scenarios, probabilities and formulas. Results were structural, not cosmetic: both models independently proposed the same missing scenario (S4 · Platform Capture, now in the report); both cut S2's weight; forecast definitions were tightened (F1, F3, F4); the simulator engine was hardened twice by independent audits (premium demand curve inverted, transition costs and severance toll added, levers decoupled, legacy repricing and Jevons volume effect introduced); and one red-team factual claim was itself caught and corrected by verification (Spain's customer-service law: passed Congress, still pending Senate — not yet in force). Divergences that survived are documented, not averaged away (see F2).

4. Epistemic labeling — every figure is a FACT (verifiable public source), an INFERENCE (derived from facts) or a HYPOTHESIS (argued belief). The simulator's model is open on this very page.

The consensus mechanism, drawn

How three frontier models produced one report without averaging away their disagreements: independent research → one synthesis → adversarial attack → adjudication with three possible outcomes. Convergence is treated as signal, divergence as information, and every red-team claim was itself subject to verification.
